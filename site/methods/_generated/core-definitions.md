## Symbolic Field

A symbolic field is the broad background of human meaning within which persons and communities live. It includes cultural, linguistic, ritual, economic, familial, religious, and social meanings.

The symbolic field is usually not the unit of analysis. The workbench analyzes sacred political orders within symbolic fields.

## Sacred Political Order

A sacred political order is a durable structure of shared meaning organized around sacred objects, values, or persons — things whose violation is collectively experienced as desecration, not merely harm. It defines collective identity, legitimates authority, organizes memory and moral judgment, and specifies the goods for which members may be asked to sacrifice.

The term builds on Durkheim's sacred/profane distinction: what a political order treats as sacred determines the shape of its sacrifice demands and the limits of its corrigibility. Things treated as sacred (a leader, a race, a constitution, a revolutionary cause, a national memory) become morally non-negotiable — violation of them triggers collective outrage disproportionate to the practical harm, which is the diagnostic marker. A sacred political order is not simply a political order with symbols; it is one whose authority is grounded in the sacred status of its founding objects or persons.

Operational indicators:

- defines a collective body organized around one or more sacred objects, values, or persons
- claims legitimacy derived from or guarded by what it treats as sacred
- ranks values with the sacred at the apex
- authorizes or constrains sacrifice in the name of what is sacred

False positives: symbols, rituals, names, affinity, traditions, rules, and brands are not sufficient by themselves. The key test is whether violation of the order's central objects is experienced as desecration.

**Why not "civil religion"?** Bellah's civil religion is too narrowly tied to American democratic symbolism. **Why not "moral order"?** Moral order (Robertson Smith, Durkheim) does not specify the political and institutional dimensions that generate sacrifice demands. **Why not "symbolic order"?** Symbolic order (the term this workbench formerly used) is accurate but does not foreground the sacred-object relationship that generates the workbench's core research question.

## Embodied Institutional Order

An embodied institutional order is the material, organizational, procedural, and bodily form through which a sacred political order gains the capacity to act.

Embodiment includes institutions, practices, roles, rituals, laws, offices, technologies, spaces, and bodies.

## Pathology

Pathology is a maladaptive condition in which an embodied sacred political order preserves itself at the expense of truth, correction, personhood, and human flourishing.

Hierarchy, authority, ritual, sacrifice, sacred meaning, strong identity, institutional continuity, loyalty, tradition, and collective memory are not pathological by themselves.

## Sacralization

Sacralization is the elevation of a sacred political order, institution, leader, people, cause, memory, or territory into an object of ultimate concern whose preservation is treated as morally non-negotiable.

Ordinary respect, patriotism, reverence, tradition, ritual, or solemn memory are not enough. The key test is whether preservation becomes morally non-negotiable and resistant to correction.

## Sacred Enemy

A sacred enemy is an opponent represented not merely as a rival, competitor, or threat, but as a contaminating, existential, or morally absolute danger to the sacred political order.

## Collective Immortality

Collective immortality is the symbolic process by which mortal individuals experience participation in an enduring collective body that appears to survive beyond their own deaths.

It is not merely group identity. It is group identity that symbolically mitigates mortality through imagined collective endurance.

## Sacrifice

Sacrifice is the costly surrender of some human good for the sake of a perceived higher good. The surrendered good may include time, body, health, conscience, freedom, speech, family, agency, life, or moral innocence.

Healthy sacrifice is bounded, proportionate, truthful, corrigible, and ordered toward life.

Pathological sacrifice is coerced, concealed, unbounded, self-mutilating, or ordered toward preserving the sacred political order at the expense of persons.

## Personhood

Personhood refers to the human being as a moral, embodied, relational, truth-capable, conscience-bearing subject who must not be reduced to material for preserving an order.

## Human Flourishing

Human flourishing refers to the conditions under which persons can live truthfully, relationally, bodily, morally, and meaningfully without being consumed by the systems they sustain.

## Corrigibility

Corrigibility is the capacity of an embodied order to receive criticism, acknowledge error, revise itself, limit its own power, and repair harm without treating correction as existential threat.

## Crisis / Pressure

Crisis is a perceived or actual disruption that threatens the continuity, legitimacy, authority, identity, or survival of an embodied sacred political order.

Code crisis as both objective condition and perceived symbolic threat where possible.

## Emergent Group Psychology

Emergent group psychology refers to patterned collective tendencies of perception, memory, defense, desire, shame, guilt, loyalty, enemy-construction, and sacrifice that arise through institutions and the persons who inhabit them.

Do not claim sacred political orders literally have minds.

## Institutional Health

Institutional health is the condition in which an embodied sacred political order remains ordered toward truth, human flourishing, bounded sacrifice, correction, and legitimate common goods.

## Common Good

The common good refers to goods that genuinely sustain the life, dignity, truthfulness, justice, and relational flourishing of persons within and beyond the order.

Claims to serve the common good should be tested against actual effects on persons, truth, justice, and corrigibility.
