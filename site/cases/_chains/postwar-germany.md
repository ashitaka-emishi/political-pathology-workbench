## Claim

The Grundgesetz placed human dignity beyond political revision, embedding a constitutional limit on collective-body claims as the structural foundation of the postwar German symbolic order.

**Confidence:** moderate-high (0.76)  
**Review status:** `argument-review`  
**Uncertainty factors:** chain-requires-expansion, single-source

Article 20 encodes memory-driven-restraint as a constitutional principle: by defining the democratic and social federal state as the protected constitutional order and granting all Germans the right to resist any person seeking to abolish it, the framers institutionalized the Weimar lesson — that legal revolution (Hitler's use of enabling legislation to abolish democracy) must be made constitutionally impossible — converting historical trauma into a positive right of resistance embedded in the highest law.

## Interpretations

**Variable:** Corrigibility  
**Mechanism:** Constitutional Containment  
**Review status:** `argument-review`

*The founding constitutional document embeds human dignity as inviolable and unamendable, directly blocking the collective-body logic that characterized the preceding pathological order.*

The Basic Law's inviolable human dignity provision (Art. 1 GG) and eternity clause (Art. 79(3) GG) are direct constitutional embodiments of high corrigibility: the postwar symbolic order structurally limits its own capacity for pathological escalation by placing individual dignity beyond collective override.

**Variable:** Corrigibility  
**Mechanism:** Memory Driven Restraint  
**Review status:** `argument-review`

*Art. 20(1)+(4) converts the Weimar trauma of legal revolution into a constitutional right of resistance, encoding the memory of pathological collapse as a positive corrective mechanism — the democratic order cannot be legally abolished, and citizens are constitutionally empowered to prevent its abolition by other means.*

Article 20's resistance clause (Art. 20(4) GG) is the constitutive memory-driven-restraint provision of the postwar German symbolic order: the framers converted the Weimar experience of legal revolution — Hitler's use of enabling legislation to abolish parliamentary democracy through formally legal means — into a positive constitutional right to resist abolition of the democratic order. Combined with Art. 79(3)'s protection of Art. 20 as an eternity clause, this creates a two-layer corrigibility architecture: Art. 20 defines the protected democratic-social order; Art. 79(3) makes it unamendable; Art. 20(4) arms citizens to defend it when all other remedies fail. The memory of the pathological order is thus encoded as a constitutional right of resistance against its repetition — the clearest example of memory-driven-restraint in the dataset.

## Counterclaims

::: {.callout-note appearance="minimal"}
**Effect:** qualifies

The Grundgesetz's constitutional corrigibility did not prevent significant postwar German pathologies, including Berufsverbot restrictions on political participation and ongoing debates about memory and Vergangenheitsbewältigung, complicating a straightforward high-corrigibility reading.

**Rationale:** Constitutional design and actual political practice diverge; high formal corrigibility coexisted with episodes of constrained dissent and contested memory politics. The current chain addresses only the formal constitutional foundation.
:::
