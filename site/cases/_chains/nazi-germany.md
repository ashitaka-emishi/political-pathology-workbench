## Claim

Nazi political language represented the national community as a sacred collective body whose preservation could override individual life and ordinary pluralist correction.

**Confidence:** moderate (0.68)  
**Review status:** `argument-review`  
**Uncertainty factors:** baynes-1942-edition-wording-unconfirmed, stackelberg-winkle-2002-edition-wording-unconfirmed

The Nazi regime eliminated corrigibility through two simultaneous legal mechanisms: the Wehrmacht oath (2 August 1934) replaced the constitutional oath to the German nation with a personal oath of unconditional obedience to Hitler and explicit readiness to die for it, removing the institutional basis for legitimate dissent within the armed forces; the Law Against Formation of New Parties (14 July 1933) legally mandated single-party rule, eliminating the pluralist competition that enables symbolic-order correction.

**Confidence:** high (0.80)  
**Review status:** `argument-review`  
**Uncertainty factors:** chain-requires-integration-with-operational-documentation

*Personal loyalty oath replacing constitutional duty + legal single-party monopoly simultaneously eliminated all institutional corrigibility mechanisms, converting legitimate dissent into personal betrayal and criminal act.*

The Nazi symbolic order scores at minimum corrigibility: the Wehrmacht oath's 'unconditional obedience' and readiness to die for the oath eliminated institutional capacity for legitimate dissent within the armed forces by converting legal obligation from constitutional duty to personal fealty to the Führer; simultaneously, the Law Against Formation of New Parties legally extinguished the pluralist competition that enables symbolic-order self-correction. Together these two legal acts in 1933–1934 completed the structural elimination of corrigibility: no institutional actor could legitimately challenge the symbolic order's direction without violating a personal sacred oath or committing a criminal act. This is the corrigibility-elimination that distinguishes Nazi Germany from the restrained-reordering cases.
