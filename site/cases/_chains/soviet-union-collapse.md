## Claim

The Soviet symbolic order collapsed when decades of institutional rigidity exhausted the founding legitimacy claims of Marxism-Leninism, and Gorbachev's reform attempts accelerated institutional disintegration rather than restoring coherence, culminating in the formal dissolution of the USSR.

## Interpretations

**Variable:** Symbolic Order Strength  
**Mechanism:** Legitimacy Collapse  
**Review status:** `argument-review`

*The Soviet symbolic order's inability to acknowledge systemic failure until too late — a decades-long suppression of correction — depleted the legitimacy of its founding claims, and the reform process that might have saved the order instead revealed and accelerated its disintegration.*

The Soviet symbolic order had historically high strength (Marxist-Leninist ideology organizing identity, legitimacy, obligation, and sacrifice across a vast institutional apparatus), but by 1985-1991 that strength had eroded to the point where the order could not survive sustained crisis pressure. Gorbachev's admission of systemic failure and the formal dissolution of the USSR mark the terminal phase of legitimacy collapse: the symbolic order could neither sustain its founding claims nor suppress the centrifugal forces its rigidity had generated.

## Counterclaims

::: {.callout-note appearance="minimal"}
**Effect:** complicates

The Soviet collapse may reflect institutional fragmentation and nationalist mobilization rather than legitimacy collapse of the Marxist-Leninist symbolic order per se — many Soviet citizens remained ideologically committed even as republics seceded, suggesting the order retained partial legitimacy while its institutional vessel failed.

**Rationale:** Survey data from the late Soviet period and post-Soviet nostalgia research suggest the symbolic order did not simply lose credibility uniformly; the collapse was also a function of elite defection, economic crisis, and nationalist institutional dynamics that are analytically separable from legitimacy per se.
:::
