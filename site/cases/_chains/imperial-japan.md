## Claim

Imperial Japanese political language encoded the Emperor's divine sovereignty and the national body (kokutai) as sacred, demanding individual self-sacrifice in service of imperial survival as the highest moral obligation overriding personal survival.

**Confidence:** moderate-high (0.75)  
**Review status:** `argument-review`  
**Uncertainty factors:** tsunoda-1958-edition-wording-unconfirmed, mclaren-1914-edition-wording-unconfirmed, chain-requires-expansion

The Kokutai no Hongi eliminated corrigibility by defining the sole legitimate mode of existence for Japanese subjects as 'casting ourselves aside' in unconditional service to the Emperor — framing individual thought and dissent not as rights but as symptoms of foreign individualism to be overcome; simultaneously, the 1941 war rescript activated sacred-enemy escalation by framing the Pacific War as necessary defense of the nation's very existence against an encircling threat.

## Interpretations

**Variable:** Symbolic Order Strength  
**Mechanism:** Collective Immortality To Sacrifice  
**Review status:** `argument-review`

*The imperial symbolic order represented the national body as divinely eternal; individual death in service of that body was encoded as participation in its immortality, making sacrifice the highest moral act.*

The Imperial Rescripts institutionalized a maximally strong symbolic order in which the kokutai (national polity) organized identity, legitimacy, obligation, memory, and sacrifice around the Emperor as a divine collective body. Individual survival was explicitly subordinated to imperial survival, and death in service of the Emperor was framed as the highest moral fulfillment.

---

**Variable:** Corrigibility  
**Mechanism:** Collective Immortality To Sacrifice  
**Review status:** `argument-review`

*The Kokutai no Hongi defined individual selfhood outside imperial service as non-existence, eliminating the basis for legitimate dissent; the war rescript activated sacred-enemy escalation by framing the war as defense of national existence — together producing minimum corrigibility.*

The Kokutai no Hongi defined corrigibility-elimination as an ideological positive: loyalty requires 'casting ourselves aside,' making individual judgment an act of disloyalty and Western individualism a contaminant to be overcome. The sole legitimate mode of existence for subjects was unconditional service to the Emperor — 'the sole Way in which we subjects may live.' This is not merely a practical constraint but a constitutive ontological claim: individual selfhood outside imperial service is framed as non-existence. Combined with the 1941 war rescript's existential framing of the Pacific War, the symbolic order produced a corrigibility floor of near-zero: no institutional mechanism existed for questioning the war's direction without transgressing the fundamental definition of what it meant to 'live' as a Japanese subject.

## Counterclaims

::: {.callout-note appearance="minimal"}
**Effect:** qualifies

The Taisho democracy period (c. 1912-1926) demonstrates significant liberal and pluralist contestation within the Imperial Japanese symbolic order, complicating a reading of kokutai strength as uniformly maximal across the 1868-1945 period.

**Rationale:** The symbolic order's strength intensified sharply during wartime mobilization; treating the full 1868-1945 period as uniformly high-strength conflates distinct phases of the imperial order.
:::
